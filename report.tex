\section{Intro}\label{sec:intro} % (fold)
As the report title suggests, this project, done under the supervision of Mr. Quoitin, has the objective of demystifying the research domain and preparing me for.

More specifically, the research field in networking which is my preferred topic in computer science, hence the choice of Mr. Quoitin as a supervisor.  

In the context of my master's degree, I will participate in an internship at ETH Zurich, more precisely, in the Networked Systems Research Group (NSG) led by Professor Laurent Vanbever.
To prepare for this opportunity, it has been decided to examine and analyse three articles from this research group, with the goal of finding a precise subject for my work.
Those three articles are distinct in topic and type.

The first one \cite{steffen2020netdice}, is a technical paper explaining the implementation of NetDice, a probabilistic network configuration verifier.  
It has been selected because formal verification is an interesting topic on which Mr. Vanbever has initially done his thesis \cite{LaurentThesis}.   

The second is a workshop paper questioning the impact of networking hardware in the power consumption of a Machine Learning (ML) cluster, arguing that it could and should be addressed.
It has been selected for its topic on green networking and its difference in style and objective from the first article.

The last, a conference paper presenting multiple ways to evaluate the power consumption of routers, and inferring their observations, proposes future research paths to reduce it. 
Its measurement-analysis-insight approach is the main reason for its selection.

As they are separate works, they do not follow a unique "red-line" throughout the report.  
However, their common theme is wired networks, of which routers and their configuration are the main examined topic.   
Appendix \ref{app:routercompo} shows a schema of the inner components of a router, which is interesting to understand, especially for Section \ref{sec:NetPowProp} and \ref{sec:FantJoul}.

The two main topics of the articles are formal verification of network configurations and green networking.

The former focuses on implementing a solution to verify if a network configuration, given a specific formal property, satisfies the property. 
Its limitations are well-known and are mainly coming from the difficulty of correctly specifying the configuration or the property.

The latter is the focus on making networking hardware more energy-efficient, because, as discussed in both the second and third articles, it is a growing interest amongst researchers, ISPs and large companies. 

A section is dedicated to each paper, and these sections are arranged by chronological order of analysis.

\section{Conclusion}
To conclude, each article is analysed to extract, from its limitations, possible research topics.

Formal verification still lacks versatility and as observed by the latency induced by the Variable Elimination algorithm, introducing modular approaches tends to decrease performance.
However, the probabilistic approach, though incomplete, is a correct and often sufficient way to verify properties in network configurations.
As suggested by recent interests \cite{LeveragingLLM, LLMNetConf}, leveraging LLMs could be a possible, though imprecise \cite{LLMBench}, way of handling network configurations.
In any case, due to the fact that LLMs are a recent technology, the exploration of their usage in this context is an interesting research opportunity.   


The insights of the second article permit us to grasp how important the green networking topic is, especially in the context of the rapid growth in the number of ML clusters.
Given that wired communication only accounts for an estimated third of the global Internet power consumption \cite{NetPerc}, with the remaining 2/3 is consumed by mobile networks, investigating the power consumption of mobile internet devices is a promising topic.  
Moreover, fostering the proposed solutions from the article could also lead to interesting energy-saving outcomes.


The last article highlights the lack of understanding of  router power consumption and the difficulty of retrieving correct power measurements and estimations.
This is indirectly linked to the objectives of the IETF GREEN \cite{IetfGreen}, a research group aiming to establish requirements and standards for energy-efficiency metrics and optimisations.  
The limitations of the article, \textit{e.g.} not considering pluggable line cards in the power model or the inexplicability of the negative $P_\textnormal{offset}$, can lead to improvements of the presented power model.

Finally, an interesting research path could be the application of formal verification to green networking, as leveraging power models to define energy-efficiency properties could enable reconfigurations to satisfy such properties, possibly at runtime as demonstrated for BGP \cite{chameleon}.

\subsection{Check implementation of waypoint property}\label{app:check}
}

Alg. \ref{alg:checkWaypoint} represent a possible implementation of the \textit{check} function for the waypoint property. 
It is not the actual implementation used by NetDice but aims at understanding the implementation of the check function. 
This idea is that, knowing the forwarding graph leads to the destination (after Lin. 4-5), if it encounters the waypoint $w$ then it lies on the path towards the destination $d$ and the exploration is stopped.
However, if $d$ is encountered without previously encountering $w$, then there exists a path where the flow $(u,d)$ does not pass through $w$, returning false. 
If all path lead to $w$, then the result will be true, as the conditions of Line 9 and 11 will never be satisfied.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
  \floatname{algorithm}{Algorithm}
  \caption{Check function for the waypoint property (See Table \ref{tab:props})}\label{alg:checkWaypoint}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Procedure{WaypointCheck}{$(V_\textnormal{fwd}, E_\textnormal{fwd}), w, (u,d)$}

		\State \textbf{return} WaypointRec($(V_\textnormal{fwd}, E_\textnormal{fwd}), u w, d$)
    \EndProcedure

	\Procedure{waypointRec}{$(V_\textnormal{fwd}, E_\textnormal{fwd}), curr, w, dest$}

		\If{$(V_\textnormal{fwd}, E_\textnormal{fwd}) ~ = ~ \emptyset$ or loop in $(V_\textnormal{fwd}, E_\textnormal{fwd})$}
			\State \textbf{return} false
		\EndIf
		\Comment{below $d$ is reachable from $u$}

		\For{each $n$ in $curr$.next} \Comment{next in $(V_\textnormal{fwd}, E_\textnormal{fwd})$}

		\If{$n$ = $w$}
			\State pass
		\EndIf
		\If{$n$ = $dest$}
			\State \textbf{return} false
		\EndIf

		\If{not RecWaypoint($(V_\textnormal{fwd}, E_\textnormal{fwd}), n, w, dest$)}
			\State \textbf{return} false
		\EndIf
    \EndFor
	\State \textbf{return} true
    \EndProcedure

  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

An attempt at reproducing the article's results on numbers of networks resources is presented in Table \ref{tab:PowAttempt}. 
In this attempt, the same formulas \cite{Demy} are used in addition to a visualiser \cite{fatTreeVis}. A clear problem of using those formulas is that it assumes an even distribution of upstream and downstream ports which isn't the case for the considered switches with 60 upstream and 128 downstream ports. The number of ports in this attempt is considered to be 188.  
The number of switches is interpolated linearly between the two found bounds of layers in which the number of hosts belong.
\begin{definition}[linear interpolation]
	Given $l$ and $u$ the lower and upper bound of layers such that\\
	$H_l$ and $H_u$ the numbers of hosts for the lower and upper bounds contains $H$ the number of host to connect.\\
	i.e. $H_l \leq H \leq H_u$ \\
	Given $S_l$ and $S_u$ the number of switches used in the fat tree topology with the lower and upper bound layers.  

	The total number of switches is :
	$$ S_{tot} = \frac{H - H_l}{H_u - H_l} \times (S_u - S_l) + S_l$$
	
\end{definition}
The linear interpolation is a considerable oversimplification, as networking infrastructures scales in discrete steps.

For example, for the 400Gbps case, which is the configuration of the Alibaba pod\cite{MLCluster}, the 15 000 hosts have to be connected with 2 links, as the switches use 200G downstream ports. 
For the calculation, due to the necessity to double the links at each host, 30K hosts are considered resulting in $u = 3$, $l = 2$ as $H_u = 1,882,384$ and $H_l = 19,208$. This leads to $S_u = 48,020$ and $S_l = 294$ giving $S_{tot} = 570.44$ rounded to $S_{tot} = 571$.
The transceivers are calculated in the same manner replacing $S_u$ and $S_l$ by $T_u$ and $T_l$ the lower and upper number of transceivers between switches.

The resulting power footprint of the computed attempt is presented in Figure \ref{fig:ownPowFootprint}.

In comparison, the attempt at reproducing the article's results is presented in Figure \ref{fig:PowFootprint}. 
The main difference is the number of switches making the number of transceivers explode in the attempt.
